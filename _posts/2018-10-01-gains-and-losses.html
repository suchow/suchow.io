Just as a bit of a preview, this idea of counterfactual reasoning plays a large part on our judgments of causality. In these problems, expected utility theory says the two options are equally good. But in reality, when there's a substantial chance of winning, people pick the more certain outcome, but when there's a miniscule chance of winning, they go for the riskier option with the bigger possible gain. --- # The reflection effect ??? - Risk aversion for gains, risk seeking for losses - Outcomes with certainty are overweighted relative to uncertain cases in both directions --- # The isolation effect ???
