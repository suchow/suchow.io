Bob and Linda are two characters on a TV show. Bob is secretly obsesesd with cats, and always has been, but he's embarrased about it. Bob doesn't want Linda to know how much he loves cats, and so he's hidden his obsession from her throughout their relationship. He pretends he doesn't care much for them, but he really does. It turns out that Linda has always known that Bob loves cats, but Bob doesn't realize that Linda knows. Bob thinks he's managed to hide his cat obsession from her, but he hasn't. Even better, Linda is aware that Bob doesn't realize she knows, and so she often uses this knowledge to her advantage. For example, every so often they'll go together to a pet store, and Linda will hold up the cutest kitten in the whole world and ask Bob if he'd like to pet it. Bob says no, not wanting to let on how much he wants to, and Linda holds it even closer to him, trying to get him to break and admit how much he loves cats. We the audience find this scenario entertaining because we understand that Linda is aware that Bob doesn't realize that Linda knows that Bob is obsessed with cats. N people decide independently each week whether to go to a bar that offers entertainment on a certain night. For concreteness, let us set N at 100. Space is limited, and the evening is enjoyable if things are not too crowded-specifically, if fewer than 60 percent of the possible 100 are present. There is no sure way to tell the numbers coming in advance; therefore a person or agent goes (deems it worth going) if he expects fewer than 60 to show up or stays home if he expects more than 60 to go. 1. Requires modeling and reasoning about the thoughts of others. 2. Most solutions are self-invalidating if adopted by the group. --- # The minority game. --- class: center, middle # Fin.
